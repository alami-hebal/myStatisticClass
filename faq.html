Basic Concepts Data Analysis Modeling

What is the difference between population and sample?

A population is the entire group you want to draw conclusions about, while a sample is a subset of the population used to collect data and make inferences.

What is a p-value?

A p-value is the probability of observing your data, or something more extreme, assuming the null hypothesis is true. A small p-value indicates evidence against the null hypothesis.

What are descriptive vs. inferential statistics?

Descriptive statistics summarize data (e.g., mean, median), while inferential statistics draw conclusions about a population based on sample data (e.g., hypothesis tests, confidence intervals).

What is statistical significance?

Statistical significance refers to the likelihood that a result or relationship is caused by something other than random chance, often determined using a p-value threshold (e.g., 0.05).

What is the Central Limit Theorem?

The Central Limit Theorem states that the sampling distribution of the sample mean approaches a normal distribution as the sample size increases, regardless of the population’s distribution.

What should I do if my dataset has missing values?

You can remove records, use imputation (mean, median, regression), or apply models that accommodate missing data depending on the amount and pattern of missingness.

How do I detect outliers?

Outliers can be identified using boxplots, z-scores (|z| > 3), or the IQR method (values outside 1.5 * IQR from Q1 and Q3). Always validate whether outliers are data errors or true observations.

What is data normalization and why is it important?

Normalization scales numerical data to a standard range (e.g., 0 to 1), making features comparable in magnitude. It's essential for algorithms like KNN and SVM.

When should I use a transformation (e.g., log)?

Transformations are used to stabilize variance, normalize data, or linearize relationships. Log transformations are common for right-skewed data or multiplicative effects.

What is the difference between correlation and causation?

Correlation measures the strength of a relationship between variables but does not imply one causes the other. Causation requires further investigation through experiments or controlled studies.

What is multicollinearity and why is it a problem?

Multicollinearity occurs when predictor variables in a regression model are highly correlated, which can distort coefficient estimates and reduce model interpretability.

How do I choose the right statistical model?

Model choice depends on the data type (e.g., categorical vs. continuous), goal (prediction or inference), assumptions, and performance metrics (e.g., AIC, R², accuracy).

What is overfitting and how can I avoid it?

Overfitting occurs when a model captures noise instead of signal. It can be prevented using techniques like cross-validation, regularization (Lasso, Ridge), or simplifying the model.

What assumptions underlie linear regression?

Linear regression assumes linearity, independence, homoscedasticity (equal variance), normality of residuals, and no multicollinearity among predictors.

When should I use logistic regression instead of linear regression?

Logistic regression is used when the dependent variable is binary (e.g., success/failure), while linear regression is suitable for continuous outcomes.
